### Why "Good Story"
The firm exists to unite major brands with top-tier creators through high-quality storytelling. Treats creator channels like television networks — each channel a "network," each recurring format a "series." The name is both a product description and a positioning statement.

### The 2025 Strategic Pivot
Rebranded from full-service 360° management to a **specialized brand-partnership studio** — one "cog" in a larger machine. Other cogs are sister companies handling merch (Fan of a Fan), back-office/finance (Boring Stuff), incubation (Building Thingz), and speaking (Good Creator Speakers).

Core thesis: *"Creators don't need managers. They need CEOs."*

## 4. Business Model

### How Good Story Makes Money
- Commission on brand partnerships it originates and closes (exact % not publicly disclosed)
- Does NOT take % of AdSense, merch, speaking — those are handled by sister companies on flat-fee models
- Positions as a specialized agency, not a 360° manager

### Core Services
| Service | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Brand partnership sourcing | Proactive outbound to Fortune 500 and mid-market brands |
| Content series development | Packages repeatable formats into "series" sellable to brand partners |
| Deal structuring & negotiation | Multi-video, multi-platform, equity-based deals; contract terms, exclusivity, deliverables, kill fees |
| IP & entertainment development | Packages creator IP for TV/streaming/film; pitches to networks |
| Brand storytelling strategy | Integrates sponsors organically — aligns brand values with creator voice |
| Creator business strategy | Long-term brand positioning, new revenue streams, platform strategy |

### The Flagship Case Study
**Airrack × Pizza Hut** — Airrack set out to break the Guinness World Record for the world's largest pizza. Good Story proactively sourced Pizza Hut as the brand partner, turning a challenge video into a premium co-produced brand narrative. This is the model they aim to replicate systematically.

### Agency vs. Management Distinction
Good Story operates as a **specialized content/brand agency** — not a traditional talent manager. Does not claim to be the creator's singular career guide, does not take % of all earnings, and explicitly encourages creators to hire multiple specialized vendors.

### Creator Monetization Streams
1. **YouTube AdSense** — CPMs $3–$30+ depending on niche. Finance/tech/business commands highest rates
2. **Brand deals / sponsorships** — $30K–$500K+ per integration at top tier. Good Story's core business
3. **Merchandise** — 40–60% margins at scale. Fan of a Fan handles this
4. **Memberships / subscriptions** — YouTube Memberships, Patreon. Modest vs. brand deals
5. **Courses & educational products** — High-margin digital products. Creator Now was in this category
6. **Speaking & appearances** — $25K–$200K+ per keynote. Good Creator Speakers handles this
7. **Licensing & IP** — TV deals, book advances, podcast licensing
8. **Equity ventures / consumer brands** — Creator-founded CPG brands (MrBeast/Feastables model)

### Key Industry Trends
- Management industry professionalizing: managers now handle 75%+ of creator brand partnerships (vs. 15–20% in 2021)
- Top creators bringing operations in-house (MrBeast, Charli D'Amelio) — main long-term threat to management firms
- MCN era is over; boutique creator-aligned firms are the post-MCN model
- AI reshaping content creation economics — premiums accrue to authentic voices with loyal audiences
- Long-form YouTube resurging vs. short-form TikTok for monetization
- Brand safety concerns pushing major advertisers toward vetted creator partners
- Creator-founded CPG brands (snacks, beverages, beauty) are the new frontier — Building Thingz's play

#### Full History and Background

Yes Theory traces its origin to January 13, 2014, when Thomas Brag created a YouTube channel under the name "Practical Procrastination," producing solo comedic and informational hybrid videos. The channel generated minimal traction throughout 2014. The group formation happened in the summer of 2015 in Montreal, Quebec, Canada.

Thomas Brag and Matt Dajer first connected at McGill University through a business consulting group for students. Ammar Kandil arrived in Montreal via a road trip from Vancouver and met Thomas at a party. Derin Emre met the group through overlapping Montreal social circles. The four recently graduated or still-enrolled university students, feeling stuck in routine by their own account, decided in July 2015 to attempt "Project 30" — doing 30 things they had never done before in 30 days, filming each one. Examples from the series include getting an ear piercing and creating a secret handshake with a mayor. By the end of those 30 days, the channel had approximately 1,500 subscribers. The channel was then renamed "Generation Y Not" (a play on "Generation Y"/millennials) in September 2015.

In November 2015, the group gained national media coverage following a public gesture of inclusivity in response to the Beirut and Paris terror attacks. In late 2015 or early 2016, Snapchat's subsidiary Vertical Networks discovered their videos and offered the group a paid content deal to relocate to Venice Beach, California and produce a show for Snapchat Discover. The group moved to Venice, California in May 2016 — their shared house there was frequently called "the 506" in their videos — and changed the channel name to "Yes Theory." The Snapchat/Vertical Networks partnership lasted approximately 18 months and provided their first approximately 200,000 subscribers as well as their initial financial foundation. After the Snapchat contract ended, the group shifted focus entirely to YouTube under the Yes Theory name.

The channel reached 1 million subscribers in February or March 2018 (announced on Twitter on March 10, 2018) and grew from under 1 million to over 3 million subscribers by end of 2018 — more growth in that single year than all previous years combined. By October 2019, at the time of a Cherwell (Oxford University) interview, the channel had 4.3 million subscribers and over 450 million total views. As of mid-2026, the channel has approximately 9.9 million subscribers and approximately 1.27–1.28 billion total lifetime views across approximately 435–451 uploaded videos.

The group's central operating philosophy is encapsulated in the motto "Seek Discomfort," which became the name of their clothing brand and secondary YouTube channel.

Emphasis moved from raw country-count travel to stories about specific cultures, people, and underreported places. Covered the Ukraine war zone in September 2024 (Lviv to Kyiv to Kharkiv frontlines). #### Controversies

**1. Pseudonym and Jewish identity**
Drew's use of "Binsky" over "Goldberg" has been discussed in Jewish media outlets including the Times of Israel, Jewish Telegraphic Agency, and The Forward. His 2017 Pyongyang visit was arranged through Koryo Tours, a commercial operator running organized tours into North Korea. In his post-visit blog he spoke positively of Koryo Tours and offered to connect readers with them, without directly addressing ethical concerns about supporting the regime through tourism revenue. No specific large-scale formal backlash directed uniquely at Drew Binsky on this issue was found in available sources. He is now banned from re-entering North Korea (confirmed: December 2023 video); the specific reason for the ban is not detailed in available sources.

A travel blogger writing for OhHeyWorld published a formal critique of a Drew Binsky video featuring Christian Betzmann as an English-teaching volunteer, arguing the video did not address whether Betzmann held teaching qualifications (TEFL certification, experience with children). When followers raised voluntourism ethics concerns in comments, Betzmann responded with "WHAT BULLSHIT," which Binsky "liked" — described by the critic as "disappointing and confusing." No large-scale public backlash resulted from this. **(Single source — OhHeyWorld blog; unverified whether this generated wider coverage.)**

#### Full History and Background

**Matthew Robert Patrick — Early Life and Education**

Matthew Robert Patrick (universally known as "MatPat") was born November 15, 1986, in Medina, Ohio. He is of Polish, Czech, and Slovak ancestry and is an only child. He was valedictorian of his high school graduating class and achieved a perfect SAT score of 1600.

Patrick attended Duke University, graduating in 2009 (T'09). He earned a Bachelor of Arts with a double major in theater and psychology with a concentration in neuroscience. (Note: several secondary sources describe the degree as "neuroscience and musical theater," but the Duke Theater Studies alumni page and Wikipedia specify theater plus psychology/neuroscience concentration; the Duke primary source is treated as authoritative here.) At Duke, he met his future wife Stephanie Cordato in a video game programming class; together they created a Legend of Zelda parody called "The Epic of Stu."

**Pre-YouTube Career**

After graduating in 2009, Patrick moved to New York City to pursue acting and musical theater. The Duke alumni profile documents his work "acting and directing along the East Coast (2009–2012)" with credits in off-Broadway assistant directing, lighting design, and regional theater at Mt. Washington Valley Theatre and Peterborough Players. He ultimately concluded that acting was not financially viable.

In 2013, Patrick was hired by Defy Media as head of audience development, a role he held for approximately one year. Defy Media had been formed in 2013 from the merger of Alloy Digital and Break Media. He later publicly accused Defy Media of stealing $1.7 million from YouTubers, alleging the company ran a Ponzi scheme using creators' revenue to attract outside investment.

Also in 2013, Patrick and Stephanie co-founded Theorist Media, initially as a consulting firm. By 2016, Theorist Media had a full client roster including Fortune 500 companies. The company's main office is in Raleigh, North Carolina, with some production presence in Los Angeles, California.

**Channel Origin**

The YouTube channel was created on August 22, 2009, under the name "MatthewPatrick13," originally featuring clips from his theater auditions and performances. Uploads stopped mid-2009. In early 2011, while unemployed and job searching post-acting career, Patrick conceived Game Theory. He described it as "something to do in between sending out resumes" and as a creative portfolio showcase. He credits a specific March 2011 Extra Credits episode titled "Tangential Learning — How Games Can Teach Us While We Play" as the direct inspiration; he wanted to create "gaming's tangential learning experience."

The first Game Theory video was uploaded April 18, 2011, titled "Game Theory: Is Chrono Trigger's Time Travel Accurate?" — an in-depth analysis applying quantum mechanics and time-travel physics to the 1995 SNES RPG Chrono Trigger.

**Corporate Sale to Lunar X**

On December 20, 2022, MatPat and Stephanie sold a majority stake in Theorist Media to Lunar X, a digital media startup founded in 2021 by CEO Lucas Kollmann (a Forbes 30 Under 30 alumnus with prior private equity experience at KKR, Blackstone, and General Atlantic), with approximately 60 employees across offices in Los Angeles, London, Berlin, and Madrid. Other key Lunar X leaders include SVP Rachel Taylor (formerly WildBrain Spark, Sony Pictures, 20th Century Fox) and SVP/showrunner Rafi Nizam (formerly Sony Pictures, NBCUniversal, BBC). Financial terms were not disclosed; MatPat stated Theorist Media had received "no less than ten" acquisition offers before selecting Lunar X. Post-acquisition, MatPat remained CEO and Stephanie remained COO, and the full team stayed intact. MatPat's public quote on the deal: "Steph and I just sold Theorist. We sold our business. This channel is no longer just my channel." The launch of the Style Theory channel in early 2023 was supported by Lunar X's expanded production infrastructure, which was cited as a key rationale for the acquisition.

**MatPat's Retirement**

On January 9, 2024, MatPat uploaded a 25-minute tearful video titled "Goodbye Internet" announcing his retirement as on-air host of the Theory channels. The video garnered approximately 9.5 million views in its first 18 hours and approximately 17 million views by early February 2024. He cited wanting to spend more time with his son Oliver and wife Stephanie, and to escape "the pressures of incessant work." His final theory video was uploaded March 9, 2024, for which he created six different endings for fans to find; that video exceeded 9 million views. At retirement, he had produced nearly 3,000 videos across all channels over 13 years. His final GTLive stream aired October 26, 2024.

Post-retirement, four new creative directors took over the four main theory channels simultaneously (detailed under each channel below).

**Personal Life**

Stephanie Cordato Patrick and Matthew Patrick married on May 19, 2012, in North Carolina. Stephanie graduated from Duke in 2009 (T'09) and holds a Master of Science in Pharmacology (the specific institution is unverified). She served as research and business partner and co-founder of Theorist Media from the beginning, holding the title of COO after the Lunar X acquisition. The couple's son Oliver was born in July 2018. #### Content Niches and How They Have Evolved

**Game Theory (The Game Theorists)**

The core niche is applying real-world science, mathematics, history, and logic to video game questions — lore, mechanics, fictional universes, and in-game physics. The tagline is "It's just a theory — a GAME Theory!" MatPat was directly inspired by an Extra Credits video about games as teaching tools and sought to create "gaming's tangential learning experience." Early topics were niche academic-style gaming analyses (e.g., quantum mechanics applied to Chrono Trigger). The channel became culturally dominant through its Five Nights at Freddy's lore-theory series (68 total FNAF videos) and Undertale analysis. By the Modern Era (2018–2024), production quality increased substantially and the content broadened to cover major franchises including Pokémon, Mario, The Legend of Zelda, and horror games generally. Under current host Tom Robinson (2024–present), the channel has tilted toward trending titles such as Poppy Playtime.

**Film Theory (The Film Theorists)**

Applies the same structured science/logic/lore essay format to films, television series, and web series — covering hidden meanings, plot holes, character theories, and the real-world science or logic behind fictional events. The channel launched in 2014 with "Film Theory: Harry Potter ISN'T The Chosen One?" as its first video. Disney paid MatPat to produce at least one commissioned Film Theory video — an analysis of Scrooge McDuck's wealth. A separate named sub-series, Film Legends, launched February 18, 2017, with the premiere episode "Is Captain America: Civil War the Best Movie Ever?!" (single source; appears more opinion/ranking-based than science-driven; unverified from a second independent source).

**Food Theory (The Food Theorists)**

Applies the same essay format to food science, fast food industry practices, culinary history, food brand lore, and debunking food myths. MatPat stated the channel was first planned as early as 2017 but took until 2020 to fully launch. Content has included investigations of fast food industry secrets and food brand mascot lore. The channel uploads at approximately one video per week.

**Style Theory (The Style Theorists)**

Applies the theory format to fashion history, beauty industry claims, whether fictional character outfits would be practical in real life, and cultural style trends. The channel specifically targets female viewers and represented Theorist Media's most explicit attempt to expand the network's audience demographics. The first-ever episode was "Style Theory: The Great Pocket Conspiracy!" (single fan wiki source; unverified). Current host Amy Roberts brought a shoppable runway show to VidSummit in what was described as a "Creators in Fashion" event.

**GTLive**

GTLive has always been an unscripted live gameplay and reaction stream channel, co-hosted during the MatPat era with Stephanie Patrick. Initially a 1-month experiment launched on August 26, 2015 (streaming five days per week), it grew within 14 months to become what one source describes as the largest regular YouTube livestream, with up to 50,000 concurrent viewers per stream (single corroborating source; unverified from a second independent source). Under current host Ash (from October 28, 2024), the channel expanded beyond indie horror to broader game genres and began simultaneous streaming on Twitch and YouTube. The first solo Twitch stream under Ash was January 29, 2025.

**Content Era Taxonomy (Game Theory)**

The Theorists Wiki introduced an era classification system for the Game Theory channel's history, first referenced in the 2021 episode "3 New FNAF Security Breach Theories!":

- Slideshow Era (2011–2012): Low-production slideshow presentations; niche academic-style analysis
- Puppet Era (2012–2015): Shift to hand-drawn animations, manipulated PNG characters, and puppet-style graphics; development of signature comedic tone
- Dual Channel Era (2015–2018): Network expansion with Film Theory launch and GTLive; FNAF and Undertale theories define the channel's cultural peak
- Modern Era (2018–2024): Higher production quality, broader franchise coverage, Food Theory and Style Theory launches, Lunar X acquisition
- Tom Era (2024–present): New host Tom Robinson; network expansion to Spotify and Twitch

#### Recurring Video Formats and Named Series

**Game Theory (The Game Theorists)**
The core recurring format is a structured essay-style video applying real-world science, math, or history to a video game question. Episodes typically run 15–40 minutes. The structure follows: clickbait-style thumbnail featuring a shocked or expressive host face; title formatted as "Game Theory: [provocative claim or question]"; cold-open hook to establish the question; sponsor segment; structured argument with custom motion graphics, visual evidence, and data; comedic asides throughout; conclusion that either resolves the question or introduces a deliberate unresolved mystery or twist. Opening catchphrase: "Hello Internet, welcome to Game Theory!" Closing signature: "Remember, it's just a theory — a Game Theory! Thanks for watching."

**FNAF Theory Sub-Series (The Game Theorists)**

#### Production Style and Team/Crew

Videos follow a consistent format across all four theory channels: host presents a pop-culture question and uses real-world science, math, or logic to argue toward a conclusion. Episodes are heavily edited with custom motion graphics, animated sequences, and energetic music. Thumbnails use clickbait-style shocked or expressive host faces. All main theory channel titles follow the format "[Channel Name] Theory: [Provocative Claim or Question]!" Content has consistently been kept family-friendly, with no profanity in scripted or live content.

Early Game Theory episodes were produced almost entirely by MatPat, with each episode requiring approximately 100 hours of work from concept to publication. Growth of "Team Theorist" added dedicated animators, illustrators, editors, and writers over time. Editor Ronnie Edwards was credited as an early key collaborator enabling more ambitious animation. Jason Parker was the first full-time team member hired alongside the Film Theorists launch in 2014. By 2024, a lead editor named Ty had been promoted to overseeing both Game Theory and Film Theory.

As of July 2024, the company had approximately 22 full-time staff and "around 20 freelancers" (total approximately 44 employees per one data aggregator). The company's main office is in Raleigh, North Carolina, with some Los Angeles production presence.

The four current creative director hosts — Tom Robinson (Game Theory), Forrest "Furst" Lee Collins (Film Theory), Santiago "Santi" Massa (Food Theory), and Amy Roberts (Style Theory) — were each long-time contributors to the network before assuming lead hosting roles. Tom Robinson previously worked as a writer and was part of what was described as the internal "FNAF Boys" group; he immigrated from the United Kingdom to work at the Raleigh office in spring 2025. Forrest Lee Collins had been an early contributor/editor on the Theorist channels since the Film Theorists' inception. Santi Massa had joined the team roughly a year before the March 2024 transition. GTLive host Ash (surname unverified) was formerly a technician/crew member on the show and spent approximately one month co-hosting with MatPat before taking over solo on October 28, 2024.

#### Controversies

**1. Research Accuracy and Methodology (ongoing)**
A persistent and widely documented critique is that MatPat presents entertainment-driven speculation using selective or oversimplified science and math while framing it as rigorous analysis, and that he ignores in-universe context from games and films in favor of real-world analogues that produce more dramatic conclusions. Streamer xQc publicly criticized a MatPat video about Twitch, stating that "the lack of research in a lot of things were a f***ing disaster" and calling out significant misinformation in the video. Scott Cawthon directly called out MatPat's FNAF theories as incorrect on at least one public occasion.

**2. Plagiarism and Attribution Accusations**
In January 2019, LegalEagle (a lawyer-YouTuber) publicly accused MatPat's team of lifting arguments from LegalEagle's Fortnite copyright video (December 30, 2018) without credit. LegalEagle concluded it did not rise to legal plagiarism but called it "ripping off" a smaller creator; MatPat's team did not publicly provide credit. Separately, a Tumblr user named Della (micer2012) alleged that MatPat's team plagiarized three of her Tumblr posts about Minecraft for a Game Theory video that received nearly 6 million views, with the team never publicly acknowledging or crediting her.

**3. Clickbait Admission**
The Game Theory team admitted on Twitter on at least one documented occasion that they were "misleading on purpose to 'get views for this less famous game,'" which was cited as an instance of acknowledged deceptive titling and thumbnailing.

**4. COVID-19 Cancellation Attempt (December 2020)**
During the December 2020 St. Jude charity livestream, a cancellation push emerged targeting MatPat over a past video titled "Who Will Survive [CENSORED] Virus?" in which he had speculated which video game characters would survive a pandemic, which critics called flippant given the COVID-19 context. The cancellation push failed, attributed largely to goodwill generated by the simultaneous charity stream's success.

**5. Scott Cawthon Political Donations Response (2021)**
When FNAF creator Scott Cawthon's donations to anti-LGBTQ political candidates were publicly exposed in 2021, MatPat addressed the controversy in a special Game Theory episode titled "We Need To Talk About FNAF." He argued Cawthon was likely a single-issue voter and that FNAF as a franchise had grown beyond its creator. Many viewers felt this response was insufficiently critical and effectively defended Cawthon.

**6. FNAF ARG Doxxing Concern**
A Game Theory video suggesting a real-life ARG for FNAF fans led to community concerns about potential doxxing of individuals connected to the ARG.

**7. Defy Media Accusations**
MatPat publicly accused former employer Defy Media of stealing $1.7 million from YouTubers, alleging the company ran a Ponzi scheme using creators' revenue to attract outside investment. This predates the Theorist Media network's major growth period but represents a significant public professional dispute in MatPat's history.

#### Content Niches and Evolution

Marko's content occupies the intersection of DIY/craft, sneaker and streetwear culture, celebrity gifting, and challenge/stunt formats. His evolution across three broadly distinguishable phases:

**Phase 1 — Tutorial-Focused Custom Art (2017–2019)**

Early videos were step-by-step tutorials for customizing sneakers and clothing. The debut video, "LOUIS VUITTON x VANS OLD SCHOOL - (Full Tutorial)," established the instructional format. Audiences watched primarily for craft technique. The channel was bedroom-based, and Marko's tools included paints, markers, crayons, and other art supplies.

**Phase 2 — Broader Customization and Celebrity Gifting (2019–2020)**

Marko shifted toward a surprise-and-reaction format: creating custom pieces and gifting them to celebrities and fans on camera. This period produced his first major viral milestone ("Custom CRAYON Shoes!!" — first video to reach 3 million views). He collaborated with Addison Rae and Logan Paul during this era and significantly increased posting output, which was noted by Tubefilter at the time of his CAA signing.

**Phase 3 — High-Concept Custom Builds and Challenge/Stunt Formats (2020–present)**

Marko expanded the subject matter of his custom art beyond sneakers to cars, bedrooms, iPhones, skateboards, and e-bikes. He introduced "asking strangers" formats and large-scale challenge videos. His signature sports ball shoe construction technique — building functional wearable sneakers from used basketballs, rugby balls, tennis balls, and baseballs by cutting, gluing, stitching, and pressing the materials — became internationally recognized in footwear media circles, with coverage from Micam Mag and theawesomer.com. This phase also included a transition to professional television production via his Tubi streaming series.

#### Full History and Background

#### Content Niches and How They've Evolved

Lee's YouTube presence has passed through two distinct phases with a clean break between them.

**Phase 1 (approximately 2010–2011 through pre-2025):** A tech and consumer electronics review channel producing more than 130 videos on subjects including headphones and iPhone cases. Accumulated roughly 4,500 subscribers. No sports journalism content. No gradual pivot — the channel sat dormant during his professional journalism career.

**Phase 2 (April 2025–present): Morning Announcements.** A complete reboot of the same channel under a new brand and entirely new editorial identity. The core niche is independent, long-form sports journalism on YouTube, explicitly differentiated from hot takes, gambling-adjacent content, and traditional TV sports punditry. Lee frames the channel as restoring storytelling to the center of sports media.

Recurring thematic pillars identified across confirmed videos and reporting:
- **Sports media criticism:** The broken business models of legacy sports television, the proliferation of gambling advertising and its corrosive effect on editorial independence, corporate consolidation, and the displacement of storytelling by takes-driven formats.
- **Fan experience and economics:** The rising cost of being a sports fan — streaming fragmentation, ticket pricing, parking, beer prices, merchandise quality decline, gambling ads saturating broadcasts.
- **First-person athletic immersion journalism:** Lee participates in professional sports challenges (facing MLB pitching) as a method of building empathy for athletes and critiquing commentators who have never experienced what they analyze.
- **Sports business investigation:** Examining how corporate deals and licensing monopolies (e.g., Fanatics) have degraded the quality of official fan merchandise.
- **Sports and culture intersection:** The role of sports fandom in identity, community, and mental health.

Lee covers MLB, NBA, NFL, Olympics, and PGA based on his background and stated content pillars on joon.me. His published cross-platform writing in 2025 — including New York Times and Boston Globe op-eds — functions as companion content reinforcing the same thematic frame.

#### Content Niches and Evolution

The show's stated mission is to feature "the most heretical thinkers and ideas of our time" through long-form interview conversations covering "unorthodox and contrarian views in science, philosophy, geopolitics, and the nature of reality."

Primary content pillars include: UFOs/UAP and government non-disclosure; non-human intelligence; consciousness studies and simulation theory; psychedelics and altered states research; ancient civilizations and hidden history; frontier physics and antigravity; remote viewing and classified government programs. Secondary and adjacent topics include AI and technology, geopolitics, religion and metaphysics, and occult history (including episodes on Jack Parsons and the occult history of space exploration).

The content has evolved through at least three discernible phases. From approximately 2021–2022, episodes were broader "contrarian science and philosophy" explorations, exemplified by early Garry Nolan and Eric Weinstein conversations. From 2023 onward, the channel shifted toward an increasingly UAP-disclosure-focused lens, directly tracking David Grusch's June 2023 public disclosure, July 2023 congressional testimony, and the broader mainstream UAP moment — the co-produced Grusch/Yes Theory video in October 2023 was the inflection point. By 2025–2026, episode titles employ more aggressive sensationalist framing ("BREAKING: Brazil's Defense Minister Says They've Had Alien Contact"; "'I Saw a Mantis Alien At THIS American Military Base!'") while maintaining 2–5 hour long-form depth. The channel also periodically ventures outside pure UAP territory into consciousness science (Donald Hoffman), ancient catastrophe (Graham Hancock), and broader heterodox or conspiracy-adjacent territory (Alex Jones), suggesting the operating frame is a "heretical ideas" umbrella rather than strictly UAP.

#### Recurring Video Formats and Series

No formally named sub-series or season-based structure has been identified. All content appears under the single "American Alchemy" umbrella. However, several recurring structural formats are evident across the episode catalog:

**Long-Form Solo Interview (core format):** The dominant format, comprising the majority of episodes. A single guest (occasionally two) in a conversation running 2–5 hours. Titles combine clickbait-style sensationalism with guest credentials (e.g., "'Pentagon's UFO Investigator: We're Not Ready For This'"). Covers one topic area in depth with minimal editing interruptions during the conversation itself.

**Documentary/Multi-Guest Hybrid:** A more produced format featuring multiple guests, editorial narration, and documentary-style assembly. Exemplified by "The Lue Elizondo Documentary," which features appearances from Lue Elizondo, Hal Puthoff, Eric Weinstein, David Grusch, Jacques Vallée, Garry Nolan, and Ross Coulthart assembled into a single edited production rather than a linear conversation.

**Two-Part/Multi-Part Episodes:** Used for conversations that exceed even the show's typically long runtime. The Graham Hancock appearance was split into at least two parts: "Graham Hancock: Aliens, Atlantis & the Apocalypse" and "Graham Hancock: UFOs, the Pyramids, and Ancient Cataclysms (Part 2)" — both confirmed on IMDb as 2024 episodes.

**Immersive Access/Embed Format ("I Spent X Hours/Days With…"):** Exemplified by "I Spent 48 Hours With Bob Lazar: Area 51's UFO Whistleblower" (April 13, 2026), which involved a multi-day visit and combined access footage with a formal interview alongside filmmaker Luigi Vendettelli and NASA researcher Dr. Charles Buhler. This format suggests extended field production rather than a studio sit-down.

**Clips Channel:** Short-form excerpts from longer episodes distributed on the separate "Jesse Michels Clips" YouTube channel.
